# Location System — The Geography of Power and Desire

## Overview

Every space in the ton carries meaning. A ballroom is an arena. A garden is a confessional. A private study is a battlefield. This file defines the major locations of the Bridgerton world, what happens in each, who frequents them, and — critically — the **privacy level** that determines how much scandal a given space can generate.

---

## PRIVACY LEVELS

| Level | Label | Description | Scandal Multiplier |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Full Public** | Everyone sees everything. Dozens or hundreds of witnesses. A ballroom floor, the Promenade. | Any indiscretion is ×2 severity — you performed it on a stage. |
| **2** | **Semi-Public** | A subset sees. A theatre box, a supper table, a corner of a drawing room. Some witnesses, but not the whole ton. | Standard severity. Containment is possible if witnesses are managed. |
| **3** | **Semi-Private** | Observed by few. A terrace during a ball, a quiet corridor, a garden path within earshot. The danger zone — private enough to tempt, public enough to ruin. | Scandal severity depends entirely on WHO witnesses. A sympathetic friend? Manageable. Lady Featherington? Catastrophic. |
| **4** | **Private** | No witnesses (probably). A closed study, a bedchamber, the back room of a modiste's shop. But "private" in the ton is a lie — servants see everything, walls are thinner than reputations, and someone always knows. | No immediate scandal — unless discovered. But secrets in the ton have a half-life, and privacy is borrowed time. |
| **5** | **Truly Private** | Genuinely unobserved. Rare. A letter read alone. A midnight walk on a country estate with no one for miles. The inside of your own head. | No witnesses, no scandal — unless you confess, or the evidence is found later. |

## MAJOR LOCATIONS

### 1. THE BRIDGERTON HOUSE — Grosvenor Square

**Description:** Warm, elegantly appointed, slightly chaotic with the energy of eight siblings and their expanding families. The wallpaper is impeccable. The noise level is not. Under Violet's management, it became the ton's gold standard for a family home — welcoming without being permissive, grand without being cold. Now jointly managed by Violet, Anthony, and Kate.

**Privacy Level:** 2-3 (Semi-Public to Semi-Private, depending on room)

**Who Frequents:** The Bridgerton family, their spouses, close allies (Lady Danbury, the Mondriches). Morning callers during receiving hours. Suitors under Anthony's terrifying scrutiny.

**What Happens Here:**
- **Drawing Room:** Formal calls, tense family meetings, the receiving of invitations and bad news. Where the Bridgertons present their public face. Privacy Level 2.
- **The Garden:** Large enough for walking, secluded enough for conversation the servants shouldn't hear. Where Bridgerton romances tend to accelerate. Privacy Level 3-4.
- **The Study:** Anthony's domain. Negotiations, confrontations, the rare genuine conversation between brothers. Where Anthony has both proposed business alliances and challenged men to duels. Privacy Level 4.
- **The Kitchens & Servants' Quarters:** The information highway. Everything said upstairs reaches downstairs within the hour. The Bridgerton servants are loyal but not deaf.

**Narrative Potential:** The Bridgerton house is where the player encounters the family at their most real — alliances form here, romances deepen, and the family's collective judgment can make or break a newcomer's Season.

---

### 2. THE FEATHERINGTON HOUSE — Adjacent to Grosvenor Square

**Description:** Louder than the Bridgertons' in every way. The décor under Portia is maximalist — bold colours, ostentatious furnishings, everything designed to project wealth that may or may not exist. The house itself is a performance, just like its matriarch.

**Privacy Level:** 2-3 (but nothing is truly private — Portia has ears in every wall)

**Who Frequents:** The Featherington family, their suitors (carefully vetted by Portia for fortune rather than character), social climbers, the suspicious, and the curious.

**What Happens Here:**
- **Drawing Room:** Portia's throne room. She receives callers with the precision of a general reviewing troops, evaluating every visitor for utility. Privacy Level 2.
- **Penelope's Chamber:** Now rarely occupied since her marriage — but once the birthplace of Lady Whistledown's pages. Privacy Level 4-5.
- **The Garden:** Smaller than the Bridgertons', more overlooked. A place for whispered conspiracies between Featherington schemes. Privacy Level 3.

**Narrative Potential:** The Featherington house is where schemes are hatched, debts are concealed, and Portia assesses whether you can be used. Visiting here signals either social obligation, romantic interest in a Featherington, or descent into the family's web.

---

### 3. DANBURY HOUSE — Mayfair

**Description:** Immaculate, dignified, and exactly as intimidating as its occupant intends. Every piece of furniture has been in the family for three generations or was selected by Lady Danbury herself to project absolute authority. Her cane has its own resting place in every room.

**Privacy Level:** 3-4 (Lady Danbury sees all, but she is discreet about what she reveals)

**Who Frequents:** Lady Danbury. Those she has summoned — which is a different thing from those she has invited. Her brother Marcus. The Mondrich family (her protégés). Queen Charlotte (as an equal, not a visitor).

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Receiving Room:** Where Lady Danbury holds court. Being summoned here is either an honour or a sentencing, and you will not know which until you are seated. Privacy Level 3.
- **The Private Study:** Where real conversations happen. If Lady Danbury invites you here, she has decided you are either worth investing in or worth warning. Privacy Level 4.

**Narrative Potential:** An invitation to Danbury House means Lady Danbury has noticed you. Her approval is worth more than a royal endorsement; her disapproval closes doors you didn't know existed.

---

### 4. BUCKINGHAM PALACE (THE QUEEN'S COURT)

**Description:** Vast, gilded, deliberately overwhelming. Every surface screams Crown authority. The throne room alone could swallow a country house. Queen Charlotte presides here with Brimsley at her side — an audience with the Queen is a performance reviewed by the most critical eye in England.

**Privacy Level:** 1 (Full Public — the entire court watches)

**Who Frequents:** Queen Charlotte, Brimsley, the court, debutantes during Presentation, anyone summoned by royal command.

**What Happens Here:**
- **Court Presentation:** The defining moment of a debutante's entry into society. The Queen assesses each young woman (and increasingly, each young man) and names the Diamond. The player's Presentation is their first test. Privacy Level 1.
- **Private Royal Audience:** Rare and significant. If the Queen summons you privately, something has either gone very right or very wrong. Brimsley will be present — he always is. Privacy Level 3 (fewer witnesses, but the Queen IS the ton).
- **Royal Events:** Balls, garden parties, and performances held at the palace. Attendance is compulsory; absence is noted.

**Narrative Potential:** The palace raises every stake. Nothing said or done here is casual. The Queen's favour can elevate you to Paragon; her displeasure can end you.

---

### 5. HYDE PARK — The Promenade

**Description:** The ton's public stage. During fashionable hours (roughly 11am to 1pm), Hyde Park becomes a living theatre where Society parades, converses, flirts, and surveils. Carriages process along Rotten Row. Walking paths allow side-by-side conversation that the whole park can observe.

**Privacy Level:** 1-2 (Full Public, softening to Semi-Public on quieter paths)

**Who Frequents:** Everyone. The Promenade is the ton's daily performance. Absence is noted as much as attendance.

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Promenade:** Walking, riding, being seen. Who walks with whom signals alliance, courtship, or conspiracy. A gentleman offering his arm is a public declaration of interest. Privacy Level 1.
- **The Serpentine:** The lake's edge offers slightly more seclusion — enough for a private word, not enough for a private act. Privacy Level 2.
- **Remote Paths:** Shaded walks further from the main promenade where genuine conversation — or genuine danger — becomes possible. Privacy Level 3.

**Narrative Potential:** Hyde Park is where the ton's daily social calculus plays out. Every walk is a statement. Being seen with the wrong person — or not being seen at all — carries consequence.

---

### 6. VAUXHALL PLEASURE GARDENS

**Description:** London's temple of entertainment — illuminated walkways, music, wine, masked revelers, and the intoxicating sense that the normal rules have loosened. Vauxhall operates in the liminal space between respectable and scandalous; the ton visits, but always with the understanding that what happens under Vauxhall's lanterns is subject to different gravity.

**Privacy Level:** 2-4 (ranges wildly — the main walks are Semi-Public, the Dark Walks are dangerously Private)

**Who Frequents:** The ton (entertaining themselves), rakes (hunting), lovers (hiding), adventurers (exploring boundaries).

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Grand Walk:** Music, fireworks, conversation. Respectable entertainment. Privacy Level 2.
- **Supper Boxes:** Semi-private dining areas where groups gather. Intimate enough for charged conversation; observed enough to prevent disaster. Privacy Level 2-3.
- **The Dark Walks:** Dimly lit, hedge-lined paths where the ton's rules genuinely dissolve. This is where kisses are stolen, assignations are kept, and reputations are gambled on the hope that the shadows are deep enough. Privacy Level 4+. **Warning:** Being discovered on the Dark Walks with someone is an automatic Level III scandal minimum.
- **The Masked Events:** Vauxhall occasionally hosts masquerades. Identity is veiled, inhibitions lowered, and the consequences are deferred until the masks come off.

**Narrative Potential:** Vauxhall is where desire gets permission. The setting itself is seductive — the lanterns, the music, the wine, the darkness. Scenes set here should crackle with possibility and danger.

---

### 7. THE MODISTE — Genevieve Delacroix's Shop

**Description:** A Mayfair dress shop that is far more than it appears. Behind bolts of silk and half-pinned gowns, Genevieve Delacroix runs an intelligence network as sophisticated as any spymaster's. She hears every confidence whispered during fittings. She knows every body in the ton — literally, she dresses them.

**Privacy Level:** 3-4 (private enough for confession, but Genevieve is always listening)

**Who Frequents:** Every woman of means in the ton. Penelope (a longstanding customer and Whistledown conspirator). Women seeking gowns for specific purposes — seduction, mourning, reinvention.

**What Happens Here:**
- **Fittings:** A woman in a state of undress, being measured and pinned, is physically vulnerable. Fittings create an intimacy that loosens tongues. Privacy Level 4.
- **Consultations:** Genevieve advises not just on fabric but on strategy — what to wear to signal mourning, availability, wealth, defiance. Privacy Level 3-4.
- **The Back Room:** Where Whistledown's pages were once printed. Now simply a workshop — probably. Privacy Level 5.

**Narrative Potential:** The modiste is where women prepare for battle (balls are battles) and where secrets slip out between pins. Genevieve herself is a potential ally, confidante, or information broker.

---

### 8. THE OPERA HOUSE

**Description:** Theatre, spectacle, and the ton's most public arena for private display. The performance on stage is secondary to the performance in the boxes — who sits with whom, who watches whom, who leaves together.

**Privacy Level:** 1-2 (the boxes are Semi-Public — you can be seen by anyone with opera glasses, and everyone has opera glasses)

**Who Frequents:** The ton at its most theatrical. Attending the opera is not about the opera — it is about being seen at the opera.

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Boxes:** Each family maintains a box. Seating arrangements signal alliances. Being invited to sit in someone's box is a public endorsement. Privacy Level 2.
- **The Corridors During Interval:** The intermission crush, where everyone circulates and conversations happen at speed. Privacy Level 2.
- **Behind the Stage:** Where the performers live — a different world from the audience. Siena Rosso's domain. Where gentlemen visit their mistresses and the class wall thins to nothing. Privacy Level 4.

**Narrative Potential:** Opera scenes are layered — what's on stage mirrors, mocks, or foreshadows what's happening in the boxes. The darkness of the theatre itself enables covert glances, whispered words, the hand that finds another hand in the dark.

---

### 9. MONDRICH'S GENTLEMAN'S CLUB

**Description:** Will Mondrich's establishment — a club for gentlemen that bridges the old guard and the newly titled. More relaxed than White's or Brooks's, with the quiet authority of its proprietor. Boxing trophies share wall space with portraits of peers.

**Privacy Level:** 3-4 (men's space — women do not enter, which both limits and defines what happens here)

**Who Frequents:** Will Mondrich, gentlemen of the ton, Benedict (regularly), Colin (occasionally), men who want to drink, gamble, and speak freely away from female observation.

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Main Room:** Drinking, cards, conversation without the social performance demanded at mixed events. Men speak more freely here — about finances, desires, fears. Privacy Level 3.
- **Private Card Rooms:** Where serious gambling happens. Fortunes are won and lost. Debts accumulate. Privacy Level 4.
- **Will's Office:** Where Mondrich conducts business and occasionally offers counsel. Privacy Level 4.

**Narrative Potential:** For a male OC, this is where male friendships form and where men's private worlds are revealed. For a female OC, this is a world they cannot enter — which makes it a target for information-gathering or a source of frustration at the gendered restrictions of the ton.

---

### 10. COUNTRY ESTATES

**Description:** When the Season ends (or when scandal demands retreat), the ton disperses to country estates. These are vast properties with sprawling grounds — parkland, lakes, gardens, tenant farms, hunting grounds, and more bedrooms than any one family could ever occupy. House parties bring selected guests together in enforced proximity for days or weeks.

**Privacy Level:** 1-5 (the full range — a country house party is simultaneously the most public and most private space in the ton)

**Who Frequents:** The hosting family, their invited guests (a carefully curated social ecosystem), and the small army of servants who maintain the estate.

**What Happens Here:**
- **The Public Rooms:** Drawing rooms, dining rooms, the library — where guests socialize under observation. Privacy Level 2.
- **The Gardens & Grounds:** Miles of walking paths, secluded follies, ornamental lakes. Where desire goes to breathe. Privacy Level 3-5.
- **Bedchamber Corridors:** The most dangerous geography in England. After midnight, when the candles are out, the corridors of a country house become a map of desire and risk. Who passes whose door. Who knocks. Who doesn't leave until dawn. Discovery is catastrophic — but the darkness is vast. Privacy Level 4-5.
- **The Lake / Riding Trails:** Physical activity creates physical proximity. A ride through the countryside, a swim in the lake, the body in motion — these scenes vibrate with energy that polite society keeps contained. Privacy Level 4.
- **The Hunt:** Gentlemen's sport that also serves as a theatre of masculinity — who rides well, who shoots straight, who keeps their nerve.

**Narrative Potential:** Country estate scenes are Bridgerton at its most potent — the forced proximity of a house party, the vast private grounds, the midnight corridors, the relaxation of London's strictest rules. If the Season is where reputations are built, the country is where they are tested. This is where affairs begin, where alliances solidify over brandy, where the real person emerges from beneath the social costume.

---

### 11. THE CHURCH

**Description:** Where marriages are solemnized, christenings celebrated, and occasionally, where weddings collapse in front of the entire ton (see: Edwina Sharma, Season 2).

**Privacy Level:** 1 (Full Public during ceremonies) / 4 (private prayer and confession)

**Narrative Potential:** Weddings are climactic scenes — emotionally enormous, socially definitive. A church scene should feel like a verdict being delivered.

---

### 12. THE STREETS & MARKETS OF LONDON

**Description:** Outside the ton's gilded spaces, London is a city — crowded, loud, reeking, alive. The print shop where Whistledown is published. The docks where trade flows. The streets where people who are not peers live, work, and have their own stories.

**Privacy Level:** 2-5 (depends entirely on the specific location)

**Who Frequents:** Everyone who isn't in a ballroom. Theo Sharpe's world. The servants on their days off. Gentlemen visiting parts of London they would never acknowledge in polite company.

**Narrative Potential:** Leaving the ton's bubble is both liberating and dangerous. The player encounters a different world — one where their title means less and their person means more. Romantic encounters across class lines happen here. Political awakenings happen here. The real London lives here.

## LOCATION SELECTION RULES

When setting a scene, the Narrator should:

1. **Match location to narrative need.** A seduction scene belongs in Vauxhall or a country estate garden, not Buckingham Palace. A confrontation belongs in a study, not the Promenade.
2. **Use privacy level to calibrate risk.** Low privacy = high scandal potential. High privacy = deeper intimacy but delayed consequences (secrets will eventually surface).
3. **Let the space shape the interaction.** Characters behave differently in different spaces. Anthony at a ball is commanding; Anthony in his study after midnight and three glasses of brandy is vulnerable.
4. **Move the player through spaces.** A Season should take the player through multiple locations — the variety keeps scenes fresh and creates different kinds of tension.
5. **The location is a character.** Describe it with sensory detail. The smell of beeswax candles. The cold of a marble floor through thin slippers. The warmth of a fire after a rain-soaked walk. The space affects the body, and the body drives the scene.
