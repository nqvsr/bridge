# Social System — Reputation, Scandal & Influence

## Overview

In the ton, reputation is not a feeling — it is a **mechanic**. Every character exists on a sliding scale of social standing, and every action, word, or association can push that scale upward or downward. This file defines how reputation works, what causes scandal, and how influence is gained or lost.

---

## Reputation Scale

Each character carries a **Reputation Score** that reflects their current standing in society. This score is not a number visible to the world — it is the invisible calculus by which every member of the ton judges every other.

### Tiers of Reputation

| Tier | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **5** | **Paragon** | Universally admired. Invitations are never refused. The Crown takes notice. Whispered about only in reverence. |
| **4** | **Esteemed** | Well-regarded and sought after. A reliable presence at important events. Minor missteps are forgiven. |
| **3** | **Respectable** | In good standing. Neither remarkable nor disgraced. The comfortable default for most of the gentry. |
| **2** | **Questionable** | Rumours circulate. Invitations grow selective. Other families begin to distance themselves. |
| **1** | **Disgraced** | Openly shunned. Doors close. Matches collapse. Recovery is possible but painful and slow. |
| **0** | **Ruined** | Cast out entirely. No invitations, no alliances, no prospects. Only extraordinary intervention — or years of exile — might restore a fraction of standing. |

### Starting Reputation

- Members of **established noble families** (e.g., the Bridgertons, Viscounts with generations of spotless standing) begin at Tier 3 or 4, depending on family history.
- **New arrivals** to the ton (e.g., the Sharmas arriving from India, or families recently elevated) begin at Tier 2 — they must prove themselves.
- **Families in financial distress** (e.g., the Featheringtons, whose debts, gambling scandals, and fraudulent relations have plagued them for Seasons) hover at Tier 2–3, their standing propped up by performance rather than substance.
- **Newly titled commoners** (e.g., the Mondrich family, who unexpectedly inherited a barony) begin at Tier 2. Wealth and title are not enough — the ton demands proof of belonging.
- **Servants, illegitimate children, and the working class** exist outside the tier system but have their own internal hierarchies (see Power Structure). An illegitimate daughter, even of an earl, holds no standing whatsoever unless formally acknowledged or elevated by extraordinary means.

## What Raises Reputation

### Positive Actions

| Action | Effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Receiving royal favour or praise | +1 to +2 tiers | The single most powerful boost available. |
| Making an advantageous match | +1 tier | Marrying well reflects glory on the entire family. |
| Hosting a successful event | +0.5 to +1 tier | A magnificent ball or dinner establishes one as a social authority. |
| Being named the Diamond of the First Water | +2 tiers (immediate) | Conferred personally by Queen Charlotte during the Court Presentation. Generates immense attention and suitor interest. The Queen may also name a gentleman as the season's most eligible catch. |
| Acting with conspicuous honour | +0.5 tier | Charitable acts, military distinction, defending the vulnerable — if witnessed. |
| Receiving positive mention in the gossip sheets | +0.5 tier | Public praise amplifies standing quickly. |
| Forming a powerful alliance (friendship, patronage) | +0.5 tier | Association with the esteemed elevates by proximity. |

### Important Principle: Visibility Matters

A good deed done in private gains nothing. Reputation is built in **public view**. Acts of virtue must be *seen* — or at least *reported* — to carry weight.

## What Causes Scandal

Scandal is the great destroyer. It operates on a spectrum from whispered suspicion to public catastrophe.

### Scandal Severity Levels

| Level | Name | Examples | Reputation Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | **Whisper** | A lingering glance, an ambiguous absence, a slightly improper remark. A gentleman seen calling too frequently without declaring intentions. | -0.5 tier. Easily managed if addressed swiftly. |
| **II** | **Rumour** | Being seen unchaperoned with a suitor (as when Daphne Bridgerton and the Duke were discovered in a garden). An overheard argument. A questionable association with someone beneath one's station. | -1 tier. Requires active damage control. |
| **III** | **Incident** | A public quarrel, a rejected proposal aired openly, a wedding that collapses at the altar (as when Edwina Sharma halted her wedding to Anthony Bridgerton upon realising his heart lay elsewhere). Evidence of deception. | -1 to -2 tiers. Difficult to contain. |
| **IV** | **Catastrophe** | A secret identity exposed (as when Penelope Featherington was revealed as Lady Whistledown). Being found in a compromising position that demands immediate marriage. A broken engagement. Financial ruin exposed (as with the Featherington gambling debts). A stepmother's embezzlement of a ward's inheritance uncovered. | -2 to -3 tiers. May force marriage, exile, or criminal consequences. |
| **V** | **Ruin** | An unmarried woman's pregnancy exposed (as with Marina Thompson). Illegitimacy revealed without aristocratic protection. Adultery proven. Treason. Criminal acts. | Immediate drop to Tier 0. Near-irreversible. |

### Gendered Consequences

The system is **not equal**:

- **Women** suffer far more severely from scandal than men. A woman caught unchaperoned may be ruined; a man in the same situation may merely be called a rake. When Daphne Bridgerton was discovered kissing the Duke in a garden, it was *her* honour at stake — Anthony immediately demanded a duel or a marriage.
- **Rakes** (men of questionable morals) are tolerated — even romanticised — up to a point. Anthony Bridgerton was openly called "Capital R Rake" by Lady Whistledown and suffered minimal consequence. But consistent disgrace eventually catches even them.
- A **widow** has slightly more freedom than an unmarried woman (Lady Tilley Arnold, for instance, conducted her affairs with notable latitude), but is still subject to scrutiny.
- A **married woman's** scandal reflects upon her husband and children as well.
- **Illegitimate children** carry a permanent mark. Sophie Baek, the illegitimate daughter of the Earl of Penwood, was reduced to servitude by her stepmother and held no standing until the Bridgerton family intervened and her identity was renegotiated before the ton.

## How Characters Lose Influence

| Cause | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Association with the disgraced** | Guilt by proximity. Being seen with a ruined person taints one's own name. |
| **Failed social obligations** | Declining important invitations, neglecting duties as host, breaking social contracts. |
| **Financial decline** | Creditors talk. Servants talk. The ton learns of debts, and standing crumbles. |
| **Negative press** | A scathing mention in the gossip sheets can undo months of careful social climbing. |
| **Loss of royal favour** | If the Crown withdraws approval, the ton follows without hesitation. |
| **Family scandal** | One member's disgrace radiates outward. A sister's ruin can destroy a brother's prospects. |

## Reputation Recovery

Rebuilding a damaged reputation is possible but slow and costly.

### Methods of Recovery

| Method | Time Required | Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic marriage** | Immediate (if accepted) | A powerful family may absorb the scandal by association. When Colin Bridgerton married Penelope despite her Whistledown exposure, the Bridgerton name shielded her. |
| **Royal intervention** | Immediate | If the Crown publicly forgives or endorses, the ton obeys. Queen Charlotte granted leniency when Penelope confessed her identity at a ball. The Queen also smoothed Sophie Baek's entrance into society by attending her wedding celebration. Exceedingly rare. |
| **Quiet retirement** | 1–3 Seasons | Withdrawing from London to the countryside, living blamelessly, and returning with humility. |
| **Public act of redemption** | 1 Season | A visible, selfless, or heroic act that gives society a reason to forgive. Must be genuine — or at least appear so. Penelope's public confession at the ball, owning her identity with courage, turned potential ruin into grudging respect. |
| **Renegotiation of identity** | Variable | If the circumstances of one's birth or past can be legally reframed — as when Araminta Gun was compelled to present Sophie Baek as a legitimate cousin of the late Earl of Penwood to avoid prosecution for embezzlement — society may accept the new narrative. |
| **Shifting of blame** | Variable | If the true source of scandal is exposed as someone else, the wrongly accused may recover. Dangerous gambit. |
| **Patron's advocacy** | 1–2 Seasons | A powerful ally voicing public support and reintroducing the disgraced party slowly. The Bridgerton family collectively vouching for Sophie Baek is a recent example. |

### What Cannot Be Recovered

Some stains are permanent — or nearly so:

- A woman found to have lost her virtue outside marriage (if proven beyond doubt), though even here, a marriage of sufficient power may contain the damage.
- A proven case of treason against the Crown.
- A gentleman who refuses to honour a matter of honour (duelling obligation, debt of honour).
- Financial fraud or embezzlement proven in court — as when Lord Featherington was murdered by bookmakers over his gambling debts, or when Araminta Gun's theft of Sophie's inheritance was exposed.

> *Note: "Permanent" is a strong word in the ton. With enough power, wealth, or royal intervention, almost anything may be papered over. But the stain remains beneath, waiting.*

## Influence Mechanics

Beyond simple reputation, certain characters accumulate **influence** — the ability to shape events, sway opinions, and direct outcomes.

### Sources of Influence

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **Rank** | Higher titles carry inherent authority. A duchess outranks a baroness in every room. |
| **Wealth** | Money buys entertainments, patronage, dowries, and loyalty. |
| **Information** | Knowing secrets — and knowing when to deploy them — is the subtlest and most dangerous form of power. |
| **Social centrality** | Characters who host, connect, and mediate accumulate influence organically. They become *indispensable*. |
| **Beauty or charm** | The ability to captivate a room is a form of soft power, especially potent during the Season. |
| **Fear** | Some wield influence through the threat of what they *could* reveal or do. This is fragile but effective. |

### Influence in Practice

- A character with high influence can **suppress rumours**, **secure invitations**, **broker matches**, or **protect allies**.
- A character with low influence must **rely on patrons**, **avoid notice**, or **gamble on bold moves**.
- Influence can be **spent** — calling in a favour diminishes one's reserves until rebuilt.
