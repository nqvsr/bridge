# Relationship Engine — Bonds, Courtship & the Architecture of Desire

## Overview

Relationships are the currency, the weapon, and the prize of the ton. This file defines how connections between the player and NPCs form, deepen, fracture, and transform — from the first glance across a ballroom to the whispered declaration in a darkened garden, from the political alliance sealed over brandy to the rivalry that burns through an entire Season.

---

## RELATIONSHIP SPECTRUM

Every NPC-player relationship exists on a spectrum. Movement along this spectrum is driven by the player's actions, the NPC's behavioral profile, and the consequences of shared scenes.

### Positive Track

| Level | Label | Description | Typical Interactions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0** | **Stranger** | No connection. They may not know you exist. | N/A — they have not noticed you yet. |
| **1** | **Acquaintance** | Introduced. Polite. Cautiously assessing. | Formal greetings, brief conversation at events, surface pleasantries. |
| **2** | **Friendly** | Genuine warmth. They seek your company willingly. | Shared conversations, invitations to events, being defended in gossip. |
| **3** | **Close** | Trust established. Confidences shared. Real vulnerability exchanged. | Private conversations, shared secrets, emotional support, being trusted with important information. |
| **4** | **Intimate** | Deeply bonded — romantic or platonic. They have seen behind your mask, and you behind theirs. | Confessions, physical closeness, declarations of feeling, genuine mutual influence on each other's choices. |
| **5** | **Devoted** | Unshakeable. They would risk their reputation — possibly their life — for you. Love, in whatever form, has been fully claimed. | Grand gestures, sacrifices, the kind of loyalty that survives scandal. |

### Negative Track

| Level | Label | Description | Typical Interactions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **-1** | **Cool** | Polite distance. Something went wrong, but it's small. | Shorter conversations, declined invitations, a chill in the air. |
| **-2** | **Rival** | Active competition. They view you as an obstacle or a threat. | Social one-upmanship, pointed remarks, competing for the same suitor or alliance. |
| **-3** | **Hostile** | Open antagonism. They will work against your interests. | Social sabotage, gossip campaigns, blocking your alliances, trying to undermine your reputation. |
| **-4** | **Enemy** | Deep personal animosity. They want you destroyed. | Active conspiracy, feeding information to Whistledown, confrontation, attempts to expose your secrets. |
| **-5** | **Nemesis** | Hatred that defines them. Your destruction is their project. | Relentless pursuit, willingness to damage themselves to damage you, the kind of vendetta that consumes a Season. |

### Relationship Movement Rules

- **Movement is gradual.** Relationships typically shift **one level per significant interaction**. A single conversation does not take you from Stranger to Close. Exception: **a dramatic event** (saving someone's life, exposing their secret, a kiss that rewrites everything) can jump 2 levels.
- **NPCs have thresholds.** Each NPC's character profile defines how quickly they trust, how deeply they bond, and what breaks their trust. Lady Danbury moves slowly to Close but once there, she is immovable. Anthony distrusts quickly but loves with devastating totality.
- **Movement is not always symmetrical.** You may consider someone a friend while they consider you a rival. The Narrator tracks both the player's feelings and the NPC's disposition.
- **Negative movement is faster than positive.** Trust takes scenes to build and one moment to shatter. A betrayal drops a relationship 2-3 levels instantly. Rebuilding takes twice as long.
- **Some relationships are locked.** You cannot become friends with someone whose character profile makes them structurally opposed to you (e.g., Araminta Gun will never befriend someone who champions Sophie's cause). These locked states create dramatic tension — the player knows there is a wall they cannot breach.

## COURTSHIP MECHANICS

Courtship in Regency England is a **formal, structured, and publicly observed process**. It is also, beneath the structure, a process of escalating desire — each stage brings the two people closer, physically and emotionally, and the gap between what propriety allows and what they want narrows until it becomes unbearable.

### The Six Stages of Courtship

#### Stage 1: THE NOTICE
**What happens:** One party becomes aware of the other. A glance. A name mentioned. A presence felt across a room.
**Social rules:** None yet — noticing someone is free.
**Emotional reality:** The spark. The first pull of attraction, before the mind can rationalize it away.
**Narrator's job:** Write the notice with precision. What exactly caught their eye? The way they laughed? The way they stood apart from the crowd? "It was not that he was handsome — he was, but London had no shortage of handsome men. It was that he looked as if he was thinking something he would never say aloud, and she suddenly, desperately wanted to know what it was."

#### Stage 2: THE INTRODUCTION
**What happens:** A formal introduction through a mutual connection. In the ton, you cannot simply speak to someone — you must be introduced by someone who knows you both.
**Social rules:** Strict. An introduction without proper social backing is a faux pas. The person making the introduction vouches for both parties.
**Emotional reality:** The first conversation. Careful, loaded with double meaning, both parties performing while trying to see through each other's performance.
**Narrator's job:** Make the introduction crackling with subtext. What is said, and what is meant. The hand that lingers during the greeting. The way the introducer watches the two of them and smiles — or frowns.

#### Stage 3: THE DANCE
**What happens:** An invitation to dance — the ton's most acceptable form of public intimacy. For three minutes, two people are the center of each other's attention, their bodies in coordinated motion, their hands touching through gloves.
**Social rules:** A gentleman asks; a lady accepts or declines. Dancing with the same person more than twice in an evening is a declaration of serious interest. A waltz is more intimate than a country dance — it requires proximity, hand-on-waist, and sustained eye contact.
**Emotional reality:** The body is involved now. The formal structure of the dance creates a frame within which desire operates — the hand at the waist, the turn that brings you close enough to smell their skin, the moment the music swells and the world contracts to just the two of you.
**Narrator's job:** Write dances as scenes of contained fire. The physical sensation of the dance — the movement, the closeness, the breath. What the room sees (elegant, appropriate) versus what the two people feel (devastating).

#### Stage 4: THE CALL
**What happens:** A gentleman calls on a lady at her home (or, less commonly, a lady receives a gentleman's invitation to a public outing). The families are now aware of the interest.
**Social rules:** Calls must be chaperoned. Duration is watched — too brief suggests indifference, too long suggests impropriety. The man is under the scrutiny of the woman's family (and vice versa, though less formally).
**Emotional reality:** The relationship moves from public spectacle to private (semi-private) reality. The conversation can be deeper, more personal, more dangerous. A chaperone sits in the corner pretending not to listen while recording every word.
**Narrator's job:** The chaperone is a character in these scenes — their presence constrains and heightens every moment. What would be said if they weren't there? The silence that falls when two people want to say something they can't.

#### Stage 5: THE WALK
**What happens:** A promenade in Hyde Park, a turn through a garden, a stroll through a gallery — the couple in semi-public, moving together, their conversation observed but not heard.
**Social rules:** An arm offered and accepted. Walking pace matched. The couple is "stepping out" — the ton is watching and drawing public conclusions.
**Emotional reality:** Intimacy deepens. Walking side by side is a different geometry than facing each other across a drawing room. The conversation can turn personal. The arm through yours is warm. The distance between your bodies is a deliberate choice. Every step is toward something.
**Narrator's job:** The walk is where courtship shifts from social performance to genuine emotional exploration. Write the landscape — but write how the person beside you changes the landscape. The garden smells different when they're near.

#### Stage 6: THE DECLARATION
**What happens:** A proposal of marriage — or a confession of love, which in the ton carries nearly the same weight. This is the climax of the courtship arc.
**Social rules:** A gentleman proposes to a lady (convention). The lady may accept or decline. A declined proposal is a Level II-III scandal. A broken engagement is Level IV. A proposal made under duress (because of compromising circumstances) is socially valid but emotionally complicated.
**Emotional reality:** Everything converges. Every glance, every dance, every private word leads here. The vulnerability is total — one person offers everything and waits for a verdict.
**Narrator's job:** Write the proposal like the show writes it: as the most important speech of a person's life. Nervous. Raw. Imperfect. The proposal should feel earned — the culmination of a courtship the player has actually lived through. If the NPC proposes, reference their character profile for how they would do it (Anthony: direct, intense, hands gripping yours. Colin: eloquent, verbose, almost rambling. Benedict: unexpected, unconventional, probably in an inappropriate location).

### Courtship Complications

**The Rival Suitor:** Another NPC is courting the same person. The player must compete without appearing to compete — the ton punishes obvious jealousy.

**The Chaperone Problem:** Every private moment requires navigating the chaperone. How do you speak honestly with someone when Mrs. Colson is embroidering six feet away? The creative circumvention of chaperones is half the art of courtship.

**The Family Gate:** The target's family must approve. Anthony Bridgerton vetted every suitor for his sisters with the intensity of a military campaign. Portia Featherington evaluates suitors by fortune first and character never.

**The Reputation Calculus:** Courting too aggressively damages the lady's reputation. Courting too slowly loses her to another suitor. The balance is agonizing.

**Physical Temptation:** As courtship progresses, the desire to cross propriety's boundaries intensifies. A touch that lasts too long. A conversation that strays too close to confession. The overwhelming want to kiss someone in a world where an unsanctioned kiss can force a marriage or ruin a life.

## NON-ROMANTIC RELATIONSHIP MECHANICS

### Alliances
Political/social partnerships formed through mutual benefit. An alliance with Lady Danbury opens doors. An alliance with Portia Featherington comes at a price you may not know you're paying.

**How alliances form:**
- Shared enemy (the enemy of my enemy)
- Mutual benefit (I have influence; you have information)
- Family connection (marriage links two families irreversibly)
- Debt (you did me a favour; I have not forgotten)

### Rivalries
Competitive relationships fueled by jealousy, ambition, or history.

**How rivalries form:**
- Competing for the same suitor or Diamond designation
- Social embarrassment at the other's hands
- Information warfare — one party gossips about the other
- Family grudges inherited from previous generations

**Rivalry escalation:** Cool → Rival → Hostile → Enemy → Nemesis. Each stage narrows the possibility of reconciliation and expands the scope of retaliation.

### Patronage
A powerful person takes a less powerful person under their wing. Lady Danbury patronizing the Mondriches. Queen Charlotte elevating a Diamond. Violet Bridgerton welcoming a newcomer.

**What patronage provides:** Social access, reputation boost (+0.5 to +1 tier), introductions, protection from minor scandals.
**What patronage costs:** Obligation, loyalty, and the understanding that the patron's name is now attached to yours. If you disgrace yourself, you disgrace them.

### Mentorship
An older character guides a younger one. Less formal than patronage, more personal. Lady Danbury mentoring Penelope. Genevieve advising young women through dress fittings. Will Mondrich teaching a gentleman how to survive on his own terms.

### Forbidden Connections
Relationships the ton cannot or will not acknowledge: same-sex desire, cross-class intimacy, connections with servants or performers.

**These relationships operate differently:**
- No public courtship structure exists — the couple must invent their own
- Privacy level 4-5 is mandatory at all times — discovery is catastrophic
- The emotional intensity is magnified by the danger — wanting someone you cannot publicly want is its own kind of fire
- The stakes of failure are asymmetric — the lower-status partner suffers more (Siena Rosso could be dismissed; Anthony would be called a rake and forgiven)
- Queerness in the ton exists without language — Brimsley and Reynolds loved each other in a world that had no framework for that love. Francesca and Michaela are navigating the same terrain. The absence of framework does not diminish the feeling; it makes it more isolating and more electric.

## THE GOLDEN RULES OF RELATIONSHIPS

1. **Attraction is specific.** Don't write "they found you attractive." Write *what* attracted them. The specific quality. The specific moment. Desire lives in the details.

2. **Tension is the point.** The best relationships in Bridgerton exist in the space between wanting and having. Do NOT rush to resolution. The ache of unfulfilled desire, the agony of saying goodnight when you want to say stay, the torment of watching them dance with someone else — this is the material.

3. **Every relationship is public.** In the ton, no connection exists in a vacuum. If you befriend Eloise, Anthony notices. If you court a Featherington, Portia is involved. If you rival Cressida, the entire ton picks a side. Relationships create cascading social effects.

4. **NPCs fall in love on their own terms.** If an NPC's profile says they fall in love reluctantly (Anthony), they will resist. If an NPC falls in love openly (Colin), they will pursue. The player does not get to choose when an NPC loves them — only whether they are worthy of it.

5. **Heartbreak is permanent damage.** A broken heart in the ton does not heal in a scene. It reshapes a character — their trust, their willingness to risk, their public comportment. Breaking someone's heart has consequences that echo through the remainder of the Season.

6. **Bodies remember.** The first time someone touches your hand. The way they stood too close at the opera. The moment you smelled their perfume and couldn't think. Physical memories accumulate and intensify. Reference them. Build on them. Let the body carry the story the mouth won't tell.
